Northumberland History

Tynemouth — Gateway to the Northumbrian Coast

Three kings lie buried beneath its Priory. Part of Northumberland for over 1,300 years, the headland at the mouth of the Tyne holds more history per square metre than almost anywhere in the North East.

360° panorama — King Edward’s Bay, Tynemouth. Drag to explore.

Stand on the headland at Tynemouth and you stand where Anglo-Saxon monks prayed, where Norman walls rose against Scottish armies, where Turner painted the ruins against a blazing sky, and where gun batteries defended the Tyne through two World Wars. The Priory beneath your feet holds the bones of kings. The sea in front of you carried Viking longships, Roman supply galleys, and the rescue boats that gave Tynemouth the oldest volunteer life brigade in the world.

A Northumbrian Place

Tynemouth was part of Northumberland for over 1,300 years. From the Anglo-Saxon Kingdom of Northumbria through to the medieval county, through the Elizabethan and Georgian eras, right up until 1 April 1974 — Tynemouth was Northumberland. The Local Government Act of 1972 redrew the map, creating the metropolitan county of Tyne and Wear and placing Tynemouth in the new borough of North Tyneside. It was a bureaucratic pen stroke, not a cultural one.

The headland sits at the south-eastern corner of what was historically called Tynemouthshire — an ancient parish encompassing Chirton, Cullercoats, Monkseaton, Murton, North Shields, Preston, and Whitley. Seven thousand acres of Northumberland soil, with the Priory at its heart.

Three Kings Beneath the Priory

The heraldry of North Tyneside still carries three crowns. They represent the three kings buried at Tynemouth Priory — a claim almost no other site in England can match.

St Oswine, King of Deira 651 AD Betrayed and murdered at Gilling on the orders of King Oswiu of Bernicia. His body was brought to Tynemouth. Oswiu founded the monastery in penance. Oswine was venerated as a saint, and his shrine became a major pilgrimage site for centuries.
Osred II, King of Northumbria 792 AD Buried at Tynemouth in preference to Jarrow, Wearmouth, or Lindisfarne — a measure of the Priory’s standing among Northumbrian monasteries.
Malcolm III, King of Scotland 1093 Killed at the Battle of Alnwick on 13 November 1093. His body was carried to Tynemouth Priory alongside his son Edward. Later exhumed and reburied at Dunfermline Abbey in 1115.

The murder of Oswine at the hands of Oswiu is one of the foundational events of the Kingdom of Northumbria. Deira and Bernicia — the two sub-kingdoms that made up Northumbria — were locked in rivalry, and Tynemouth became the burial ground of that conflict. The monastery that grew around Oswine’s tomb would stand for nearly a thousand years.

From Monastery to Fortress

The first monastery was probably founded in the mid-7th century, sponsored by Northumbrian royalty. The Venerable Bede records an Abbot Heribald at Tynemouth who died in 745. For centuries the monastery served as a centre of learning and pilgrimage, connected by faith and by sea to Lindisfarne, Jarrow, and Wearmouth — the great Northumbrian monasteries that shaped early English Christianity.

The Vikings came. Just as they struck Lindisfarne in 793, they raided the coast around Tynemouth. The monastery survived, but the world around it changed. After the Norman Conquest, the priory was refounded as a Benedictine house under the control of St Albans Abbey — a controversial arrangement that Northumbrian monks resisted for generations.

The castle grew alongside the priory. The headland was too strategically important to remain unwalled. By the 13th century, Tynemouth was both holy site and military stronghold. It was besieged during the Wars of Scottish Independence in 1296, held during the conflicts of Henry IV’s reign, and finally dissolved by Henry VIII in 1539. The priory died; the castle lived on.

The Painters’ Headland

The ruins drew artists like a flame draws moths. J.M.W. Turner painted Tynemouth Priory — the dramatic silhouette of broken arches against sky and sea captured exactly the kind of sublime landscape that defined the Romantic era. The American painter Winslow Homer spent 1881–1882 at neighbouring Cullercoats, producing some of the finest watercolours of his career — powerful images of fisherfolk and the relentless North Sea that were profoundly influenced by the Tynemouth coastline.

The Lifeboat Tradition

The Tynemouth Volunteer Life Brigade, founded in 1864, is the oldest volunteer life brigade in the world. It stands in the same tradition of coastal rescue that produced Grace Darling on the Farne Islands in 1838 — a tradition that runs the entire length of the Northumberland coast, from Tynemouth to Berwick and beyond. The sea gives and the sea takes, and Northumbrians have always rowed out to meet it.

The Roman Connection

Two miles upriver at Wallsend stands Segedunum — the eastern terminus of Hadrian’s Wall. Every Roman supply ship entering the Tyne would have passed beneath the headland where the Priory now stands. While no confirmed Roman fort has been found at Tynemouth itself, Roman altars and coins have been discovered in the area, and the strategic importance of the river mouth was not lost on a civilisation that understood harbours as well as walls.

Visit Tynemouth

Tynemouth Priory & Castle is managed by English Heritage. Open year-round (seasonal hours). The headland walk gives views across the river mouth to South Shields and north along the coast toward St Mary’s Lighthouse and Whitley Bay.

King Edward’s Bay — the sheltered beach beneath the Priory headland. One of the best small beaches on the North East coast.

Tynemouth Station — the beautifully restored Victorian metro station hosts a popular weekend market.
